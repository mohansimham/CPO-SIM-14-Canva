Choosing the Right Research Method

Not every question needs the same answer.

The biggest mistake in user research is using the wrong method for the question you're trying to answer. Usability tests tell you if people CAN use something. Surveys tell you what people SAY they want. Analytics tell you what people DO. Interviews tell you WHY. Each method has blind spots: surveys miss behaviour, analytics miss motivation, usability tests miss context, and interviews miss scale. The art of user testing is matching the method to the question — and knowing when you need multiple methods to triangulate the truth.

How to apply it

Before starting any research, write down the specific question you need answered. Then ask: "Do I need to understand behaviour or attitudes? Do I need depth or breadth? Am I exploring or validating?" Exploration calls for qualitative methods (interviews, contextual inquiry). Validation calls for quantitative methods (A/B tests, surveys with statistical significance). Most product decisions need both: qualitative research to generate hypotheses, then quantitative research to validate them. Never skip straight to quantitative — you might be measuring the wrong thing precisely.

Example

When Canva expanded from graphic design to presentations, the team needed to understand whether non-designers would actually use a presentation tool. They didn't start with a survey asking "Would you use Canva for presentations?" (people say yes to everything). Instead, they ran contextual interviews watching people create presentations in PowerPoint and Google Slides, identifying pain points (finding templates, maintaining brand consistency, collaborating in real-time). Then they built a prototype and ran usability tests to validate that Canva's visual-first approach actually solved those pain points. Only after qualitative validation did they launch a beta and measure adoption quantitatively.

Research Method Selection Framework

  1. Define the Question — What specific decision will this research inform? Write it as a single question. Vague questions lead to useless research.
  2. Behaviour vs. Attitude — Do you need to know what people DO (use analytics, usability tests) or what they THINK (use surveys, interviews)?
  3. Depth vs. Breadth — Do you need rich context from a few users (qualitative) or statistical confidence from many (quantitative)?
  4. Explore vs. Validate — Are you generating hypotheses (explore with interviews, diary studies) or testing them (validate with A/B tests, surveys)?
  5. Stage of Product — Pre-build: concept testing, prototypes. During build: usability testing. Post-launch: analytics, surveys, A/B tests.
  6. Time & Resource Constraints — 1 day: guerrilla testing. 1 week: usability study. 1 month: full mixed-methods study. Match method to timeline.
  7. Triangulation — Use 2-3 methods that complement each other. If interviews and analytics tell the same story, you have confidence. If they conflict, dig deeper.

Prototype Testing & Fake Doors

Test the idea, not the implementation.

You don't need to build a feature to test whether people want it. Prototype testing lets you validate demand, usability, and value proposition before writing a single line of production code. The spectrum ranges from low-fidelity (paper sketches, wireframes) to high-fidelity (clickable prototypes) to fake doors (real UI that measures intent without real functionality). The key insight: the fidelity of your prototype should match the fidelity of the question. Testing navigation? Low-fi wireframes work. Testing emotional response? You need high-fi visuals. Testing demand? A fake door with a waitlist captures real intent.

How to apply it

Start with the cheapest test that answers your question. A 30-minute paper prototype test with 5 users will uncover 85% of major usability issues. A clickable Figma prototype catches the remaining interaction problems. A fake-door test with a "Coming Soon" button on your live product measures real demand with real users (not survey respondents saying what they think you want to hear). The golden rule: never spend 3 months building something you could have invalidated in 3 days with a prototype. The best product teams have a "test before build" culture where no feature reaches engineering without user evidence.

Example

When Canva considered adding a video editing feature, they faced a critical question: would their core audience (non-designers making social media graphics) actually edit videos in Canva? Instead of building a full video editor, they ran a fake-door test: they added a "Create a Video" button to the homepage. Users who clicked saw a waitlist signup page. The click-through rate was 3x higher than expected, and 40% of clickers signed up for the waitlist — strong demand signal. Then they built a low-fi prototype of the video editor and tested it with waitlist users. The prototype revealed that users wanted simple video (trim, add text, transitions) not professional editing. This shaped the entire product direction and saved months of building the wrong thing.

Prototype Testing Ladder

  1. Paper / Sketch — Hand-drawn screens. Tests: information architecture, navigation flow, concept clarity. Time: 1-2 hours to create, 30 min to test.
  2. Wireframe — Digital low-fi screens (Balsamiq, Whimsical). Tests: layout, content hierarchy, user flow. Time: 1 day to create, 1 hour to test.
  3. Clickable Prototype — Interactive screens (Figma, Canva). Tests: interaction design, micro-interactions, task completion. Time: 2-3 days.
  4. Wizard of Oz — Real-looking UI, human-powered backend. Tests: full experience without building tech. Time: 1 week.
  5. Fake Door / Painted Door — Real button in live product, no backend. Tests: actual demand with real users. Time: 1 day to deploy.
  6. Concierge MVP — Deliver the service manually to a few users. Tests: value proposition and willingness to pay. Time: 1-2 weeks.
  7. Beta / Limited Release — Real feature, limited audience. Tests: retention, engagement, technical feasibility. Time: 2-4 weeks.

Signal vs. Noise in User Feedback

Five users told you X. Should you build it?

User feedback is full of traps. Vocal minorities sound louder than they are. Feature requests describe solutions, not problems. Users can't predict their own behaviour — they say they want simplicity but use complexity. Confirmation bias leads researchers to hear what they want to hear. The PM's job is to extract the signal (genuine unmet needs) from the noise (edge cases, aspirational answers, social desirability bias). The best PMs don't ask "What do users want?" They ask "What pattern of behaviour reveals an unmet need?"

How to apply it

Apply the "5 Whys" to every feature request: the first answer is usually a solution ("I want a dark mode"). Ask why until you reach the underlying need ("I use Canva at night and the white screen hurts my eyes" → the need is reduced eye strain, dark mode is one solution). Look for convergence: if 4 out of 5 users mention the same problem in different words, that's signal. If 1 user has a passionate request, that's noise. Weight behavioural data over stated preferences: if users say they want feature X but analytics show they never use similar features, trust the analytics. The hierarchy of evidence: what people do > what people struggle with > what people say > what people predict they'd do.

Example

Canva's most-requested feature for years was "layers panel" — a Photoshop-style layer management system. Professional designers asked for it constantly. But Canva's user research revealed the signal behind the noise: most Canva users (non-designers) didn't even understand what layers were. They just wanted to move elements in front of or behind each other. Canva built "Position" controls (forward, backward, front, back) instead of a full layers panel — solving the underlying need (control element stacking) without the complexity (managing a layers hierarchy). Usage data proved the decision right: 60% of users used Position controls regularly, while the comparable feature in professional tools was used by less than 10% of users.

Feedback Interpretation Framework

  1. Source Assessment — Who is saying this? Power users, new users, churned users? Each group has different biases and different value to the product.
  2. Frequency vs. Intensity — Is this mentioned by many users (high frequency) or passionately by few (high intensity)? Frequency usually matters more.
  3. Problem vs. Solution — Is the user describing a problem or proposing a solution? Always dig for the problem. Solutions are the PM's job.
  4. Behaviour Check — Does the request match what users actually do? If users ask for advanced features but analytics show basic feature usage, trust behaviour.
  5. Convergence Test — Do multiple data sources point the same direction? (Interviews + analytics + support tickets = high confidence.)
  6. Opportunity Sizing — If we solved this, how many users benefit? What's the impact on retention, activation, or revenue?
  7. Bias Audit — Am I hearing this because I want to? Check for confirmation bias, recency bias, and survivorship bias in your interpretation.

Validation at Scale

When to trust 5 users and when you need 50,000.

Qualitative research (5-8 users) is enough to find usability problems — Jakob Nielsen proved that 5 users find 85% of usability issues. But validating product-market fit, measuring conversion impact, or proving business value requires quantitative evidence: A/B tests with statistical significance, surveys with representative samples, or cohort analysis with enough data points. The transition from qualitative to quantitative is where many teams stumble: they either skip qualitative (building the wrong thing efficiently) or stay qualitative forever (never proving impact at scale).

How to apply it

Use qualitative research to decide WHAT to build, and quantitative research to prove it WORKS. The flow: (1) Interviews reveal a pain point, (2) Prototype tests confirm users can use the solution, (3) Fake-door test confirms demand, (4) Beta launch measures engagement and retention, (5) A/B test proves the feature improves key metrics. Each stage has a kill criterion: if the prototype test shows fundamental confusion, stop. If the A/B test shows no lift, stop. Validation at scale means having the discipline to stop when the evidence says stop — even if you've invested months of work.

Example

Canva's expansion into enterprise (Canva for Teams) required validation at multiple scales. Qualitative: interviews with 30 marketing teams revealed the need for brand kits, approval workflows, and shared templates. Prototype: clickable prototypes tested with 8 teams validated the workflow. Fake door: a "Canva for Teams" landing page measured demand (15% click-through from existing users). Beta: 200 teams used an early version for 60 days — retention was 85%, far above consumer Canva's 40%. A/B test: teams with brand kit access created 3x more designs than teams without. Each stage built on the last, and each stage had a go/no-go threshold. The enterprise product launched with confidence because every assumption had been validated at the appropriate scale.

Validation Scale Framework

  1. Concept Validation — 5-8 interviews. Question: "Does this problem exist and do people care?" Kill criterion: fewer than 3 of 5 recognise the problem.
  2. Usability Validation — 5-8 usability tests. Question: "Can people use this solution?" Kill criterion: fewer than 4 of 5 complete the core task.
  3. Demand Validation — Fake door or landing page, 1000+ impressions. Question: "Do people want this enough to act?" Kill criterion: below 2% click-through.
  4. Value Validation — Beta with 50-200 users, 30+ days. Question: "Do people keep using it?" Kill criterion: D30 retention below your product baseline.
  5. Impact Validation — A/B test with statistical significance. Question: "Does this improve key metrics?" Kill criterion: no significant lift at 95% confidence.
  6. Business Validation — Revenue or conversion measurement. Question: "Does this create business value?" Kill criterion: unit economics don't work at projected scale.
  7. Scale Validation — Full launch with monitoring. Question: "Does this work for everyone, not just early adopters?" Kill criterion: metrics degrade as audience broadens.
